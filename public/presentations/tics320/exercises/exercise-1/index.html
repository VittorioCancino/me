🎬 MarBrisa Disco

You've been hired to design the database system for a disco called MarBrisa.

The situation

🌊 MarBrisa is a popular disco that hosts weekend parties with themed nights.
🎟️ They sell tickets online in advance and at the entrance on the night of each event.
🍹 Inside, there is a bar where customers can order drinks from a menu throughout the night.
→ Your job: model this system as an ER diagram. Think carefully before drawing.
What it needs to handle

Four things the system tracks

🎟️ Ticket Sales
  • Sold online (advance) or at the door
  • Each ticket has a price and belongs to an event
  • A customer can buy multiple tickets
🍹 Bar & Drinks
  • The disco has a menu of drinks
  • Each drink has a name and a price
  • Customers can order multiple drinks per night
🧾 Purchases
  • Each purchase belongs to a customer
  • Can include multiple drinks
  • Recorded with a timestamp
🎉 Events
  • MarBrisa hosts themed parties (e.g. Reggaeton Night)
  • Each event has a date
  • Ticket sales are tied to a specific event
Design challenge

Design an ER model for MarBrisa.

Guide yourself with these questions:

❓Who are the entities?
❓What attributes does each one have?
❓How do the entities relate to each other?
❓Can something exist independently, or does it always need another entity?
🔥 Optional challenge: What if a customer buys drinks for friends? Do tickets belong to a person or just to a purchase?

Don't think in tables. Think in reality.

How they connect

The relationships

Tickets

Customer buys Ticket 1:N
Ticket belongs to Event N:1

Bar purchases

Customer makes Purchase 1:N
Purchase includes Drink M:N
👉 The relationship itself carries quantity. If you later move to tables, that M:N becomes a junction table.
What to watch for

Three things students usually miss

❌
Merging Ticket and Purchase into one entity

They represent different actions — buying entry vs. buying drinks. One is tied to an event slot, the other is a bar transaction. Merging them creates an insert anomaly immediately.

🧩
Forgetting that quantity belongs to the relationship

A purchase can include multiple drinks, and the same drink can appear in many purchases. That's M:N. In a conceptual ER diagram, quantity belongs on that relationship rather than forcing a separate entity too early.

📝
Mixing conceptual ER with table design too early

A conceptual ER model should first capture the real-world facts clearly. Additional implementation choices can come later when translating the model to tables.

Reasoning out loud

Walk it like this

1. Start with obvious entities

"Customer, Event, Drink… these clearly exist on their own."

2. Identify actions

"Buying a ticket" → Ticket. "Buying drinks" → Purchase. Actions become entities too.

3. Fix complexity

"A purchase includes multiple drinks" → M:N. Put quantity on that relationship, and only later translate it into a junction table if needed.

🎯 Notice how every piece represents something real. If you can explain it in words, you can model it.
